Project 05, Personalized Anthology
. Most book forms exist in relation to a certain typology (typography and format) each devised for different genres of context, and evolved in response to ergonomic, technological, and economic needs and wants. Some examples: fiction -> paperback, academic writing -> journal, monograph -> catalog, lists -> phone book/dictionary/menu, religious scripture -> prayer book, daily news -> newspaper. As you begin the design of your booklet, ground your approach in relation to a typology, or perhaps a hybrid of two typologies (e.g. newspaper typography in a paperback size, etc.), but no more. For the second process, I want you to use one of your texts to design two different page layouts (as spreads) that articulate the page size, text blocks, page numbers, footnotes, and running heads (if desired) and print them. Do not include imagery or headlines at this stage. Explore the compositional tension and balance within a page and across a spread (e.g. symmetry vs. asymmetry and combinations thereof). Experiment with all the traditional variables: page and text block sizes/ratios, type size, tracking, leading, word spacing, paragraph breaks, etc. Don't take anything for granted.

- Introduce 3rd process of
Project 05, Personalized Anthology
. From the layouts you designed, design 4-8 separate spreads (now including section breaks and imagery) which give us a sense of the overall visual grammar and textual content of your booklet. At this stage, do not design the cover or table of content (if you plan to include one). Decide whether it makes sense for your section breaks to take up an entire spread (dramatic, luxurious), one page (a little less so), a half-page or quarter-page (functional, conventional), or less (efficient). Do your texts start on the left or right side of the page or do they alternate as needed? What does the title typography look like and connote? Is it formal/informal, minimal/ornate, systemized/irregular? Ask yourself why in each instance. Then switch your attention to different ways of incorporating imagery and color (optional) into your booklet to make it more image- and information-rich. The imagery should build on strategies already developed in your spread, so that the posters and booklet feel related visually, part of a visual/verbal argument. Also, be cautious when introducing color since it could make or break your existing booklet structure (that's why it's optional). When I said imagery, imagery could be incorporated in innumerable ways; as background texture, as a decorative frame, as captions, as an isolated image on a page, intricately integrated with the text, at a distance from its reference, etc. Likewise, the imagery could function in different ways; abstractly in response to ideas in each text or overarching themes, or figuratively, illustrating specific references in the text. Your goal should be to devise a coherent strategy for your use of imagery as a whole.
